LESSON 1: THE MONOLITH VS. MODULAR ARCHITECTURE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What is a "Monolith"?
When you put every single line of code into one giant file, that is a Monolith.
Why is it bad? Hard to change, hard to debug, and everything breaks at once.

What is "Modular Architecture"?
We split the code into `core/` (Brain), `ui/` (Face), and `data/` (Memory).
This makes your app professional, reusable, and easy to maintain.

LESSON 4: SECRETS & OAUTH2 TOKENS (SPOTIFY)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
OAuth2 is how apps talk to each other securely. 
Instead of logging into Spotify *inside* our app, we send you to Spotify, 
and they send back a "Token." We store your keys in `.streamlit/secrets.toml`.

LESSON 5: METADATA, AUDIO DNA & PROJECTIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
**1. What is Metadata?**
Spotify has hidden data called "Audio Features." 
This isn't just the artist name; it's the song's **Energy**, **BPM**, and **Mood**.
We pull this "DNA" to calculate if your music matches your workout intensity.

**2. Scientific Projections (Predictive Analytics)**
In `Nutrition.py`, we added a weight projection chart. 
This is "Predictive Modeling." By taking your current data and applying a constant 
(500 calorie deficit), we can calculate the future state of your data.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
LESSON 6: PERSISTENCE, SCOPES & DEVICE STATE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
**1. Token Caching (The App's Long-Term Memory)**
Normally, Streamlit forgets everything when you refresh. We used a `CacheFileHandler` 
to save your Spotify token into a file (`.streamlit/.spotify_cache`). 
Now, our app can "read its own diary" to remember who you are without asking 
for your password every time.

**2. Scopes (Permissions)**
Think of "Scopes" like a VIP pass. We upgraded our scope to include 
`user-modify-playback-state`. This gives our app the specific legal permission 
to "press the buttons" on your Spotify player. Without this, we could only "see" 
the music, not "control" it.

**3. Device State (The "Handshake")**
Spotify only allows control if you have an "Active Device" (like your phone app 
open). This is a security feature. Our code has to check if a device is "awake" 
before it tries to send a "Play" or "Skip" command.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
LESSON 7: API PIVOTING & GRACEFUL DEGRADATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
**1. API Pivoting (When Rules Change)**
Sometimes, big companies like Spotify change their API rules overnight. 
In our case, they blocked access to "Audio Features" (BPM/Energy) for new apps 
starting in Nov 2024. As an engineer, you have to "Pivot." We removed the 
restricted code and switched to "Allowed" data like **Top Artists**.

**2. Graceful Degradation (Failing Silently)**
If one feature breaks (like our BPM gauge), the whole app shouldn't crash. 
"Graceful Degradation" is the art of letting parts of your app shut down safely 
while keeping the rest of the ship running. We "Safeguarded" our Spotify calls 
so the "Now Playing" and "Music Explorer" still work perfectly.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
LESSON 8: DUAL-MODE UI & USER-CENTRIC DESIGN
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
**1. What is User-Centric Design?**
It means building the app around how people actually use it. People have routines 
(Split Mode), but they also like flexibility (Muscle Mode). By offering both, 
the app doesn't "lock you in" to a rigid structure.

**2. Logic Deduplication**
We cleaned up the "junk" (like duplicate library names) so that Python doesn't 
have to waste time filtering through ghost data. This makes the UI snappier 
and the dropdowns shorter.
